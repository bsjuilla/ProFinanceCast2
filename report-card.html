What changed and why

Two decisions, one paragraph each.

The biggest mover was the retirement-contribution lift recorded on 22 April — a one-percentage-point increase in the assumed annual contribution rate. On its own that change moves the ten-year endpoint from $400,800 to $418,300. After the subsequent return-rate revision on 5 May, which trimmed the assumed nominal return from 7.5% to 7.0%, the endpoint settled at the more honest $412,000. Net of both edits, the forecast remains higher than it began the quarter; the contribution increase did real work.

The second mover was cadence on the credit card. The household held the monthly debt allocation steady at $410 across all three months, even when the freelance invoice landed in May. Avalanche math now retires the credit line in month 14; the student loan finishes in month 26. The discipline shows up not in any one transaction but in the absence of detours — no scenic interest, no balance creep, no debt-shuffling.

Next quarter focus

Three things to hold the line on.

  1. 01

    Finish the credit card. Hold the $410 monthly debt allocation, do not redirect to retirement until the avalanche path clears the balance in month 14. Interest avoided this quarter alone runs roughly $210.

  2. 02

    Raise the emergency reserve to six months. The reserve currently covers 4.8 months; adding $6,280 over the next nine months reaches the six-month line without disturbing other goals.

  3. 03

    Hold the savings rate at 30%. The lift to 30.0% is the single most reliable lever in the forecast. A return to 28% would cost the ten-year endpoint roughly $22,000; the cost of holding is, in practice, none.
