How big should your emergency fund be?

The three-to-six month rule is a slogan. It is also wrong for most of the households we work with. The honest answer is: it depends — and three numbers, not one, decide.

By the ProFinanceCast team 7 min read 20 April 2026

"Three to six months of expenses" is the most-repeated advice in personal finance. It is also one of the least carefully reasoned. It applies a single multiplier to a single number, ignores the variables that actually matter, and lands the typical household either at twice the cash they need to hold or at half. Either error is expensive. The first is expensive in lost compounding; the second is expensive the next time something goes wrong.

The honest answer to "how big should my emergency fund be" begins with a different question: what is the fund insuring against? When you can answer that precisely, the size falls out of the answer.

What the buffer actually insures

An emergency fund is not a savings account with a different name. It is a self-financed insurance policy against a specific class of event — an income interruption, an unanticipated lump-sum expense, or a combination of the two — that you have decided you do not want to handle by selling assets, borrowing at consumer rates, or asking family. That is the whole job. It is not for retirement, not for the next car, not for a sabbatical, not for the bathroom renovation. Those are different buckets and they should have different names on the same balance sheet.

This definition narrows the conversation usefully. The buffer needs to be large enough to bridge the gap between when an event occurs and when one of three things happens. Your income resumes, the lump-sum expense is paid, or you have decided you will sell an asset or draw a line of credit. Pick the longest realistic gap among those three and you have your target.

Current expenses vs compressible floor

The standard rule multiplies your current monthly expenses by three or six. That is the first mistake. Your current monthly expenses include things you would stop doing the day a real emergency arrived. Subscriptions you would cancel. Restaurant meals you would skip. The category of spending personal-finance writers used to call "fun money" and now politely call "discretionary."

The canonical household this site uses across every page spends $5,180 a month at current rates. If you ask the household what it would spend in a true emergency — rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries at the cheaper supermarket, insurance, minimum debt payments, transport, and nothing else — the figure drops to roughly $3,880. We call this number the compressible floor. It is the smaller of two real budgets the household has access to, and it is the number that should drive the buffer size, because the buffer is only ever drawn down in a compressed scenario.

The naive rule, applied to the current budget, asks for $15,540 to $31,080. Applied to the compressible floor, the same rule asks for $11,640 to $23,280. Same household, same arithmetic, different answer by roughly the price of a small used car.

The three variables that change the answer

From the compressible floor, three variables tell you which end of the band to sit at — and whether the band itself is the right one for you.

Income volatility. A salaried employee with two years of tenure at a stable employer in a non-cyclical industry needs less buffer than a freelancer whose income arrives in seven cheques a year. If you have ever had a month with zero invoices paid, your buffer needs the high end of the band and possibly two or three months above it. If you have had the same paycheque on the same day of the month for five years, the low end is reasonable.

Fixed-cost share. A household whose compressible floor is mostly rent or mortgage and insurance — obligations that do not yield to discipline — is more exposed than a household with the same total floor where half is groceries and transport. Fixed costs do not negotiate with you when the income stops; variable costs do. Households with high fixed-cost shares need larger buffers because the buffer is bridging a less compressible expense base.

Dependents. A solo earner with no dependents has fewer scenarios that demand a fast cash response. A two-earner household with one set of dependents has more, and a single-earner household with dependents has the most. The number is not just about size; it is about who else relies on the household's functioning being uninterrupted.

For the canonical household — mid-career, single earner, salaried but in a cyclical industry, mortgage as the largest fixed cost, no dependents — we recommend a target band of $20,720 to $31,080. That is roughly five to eight months of the compressible floor, sitting near the higher end of the conventional rule because the fixed-cost share is high and the industry is cyclical. The lower end of that band is sufficient if the employment becomes more stable; the upper end is the right answer if a partner stops working.

Where to hold it

The buffer should sit in an account that has two properties: it is accessible within a week, and it pays something. In current rate environments that means a high-yield savings account or, in markets that allow it, a short-duration money-market fund. It should not sit in a checking account paying nothing, because the opportunity cost compounds and the buffer is a long-term holding even though it exists to handle short-term events. It also should not sit in a brokerage account exposed to market risk; the entire premise of the buffer is that it is the asset you can rely on when other assets are stressed.

Keep it separate from your day-to-day account, both for accounting honesty and because separation reduces the temptation to dip in for things that are not emergencies. A name on the account helps. Households we have worked with call theirs "Reserve" or "Bridge" or simply "Do not touch."

When to stop adding

The hardest part of running an emergency fund is recognising when to stop funding it. Past the upper end of your target band, every additional dollar is doing one of two things. It is earning a high-yield-savings rate when it could be earning a market rate in a retirement account. Or it is earning a market rate that you could be using to pay down debt at a higher interest rate, which is the same problem in a different shirt.

When the buffer hits the upper end of the band, redirect the monthly contribution. Towards retirement if your tax-advantaged space is unused. Towards the most expensive debt if you have any. Towards an investment account if both of those are handled. The buffer is a station you stop at on the way to other goals, not a destination. The most expensive emergency-fund mistake we see is not undersaving; it is overstaying.
