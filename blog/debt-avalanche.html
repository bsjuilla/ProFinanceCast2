Avalanche vs Snowball: which gets you debt-free faster

Most readers ask the question wanting momentum. The math wants the highest interest rate. Both sides are reasonable — only one is cheaper, and not by as much as either camp claims.

By the ProFinanceCast team 8 min read 4 May 2026

Two strategies have dominated the personal-finance conversation about consumer debt for a generation. Avalanche orders your balances by interest rate, highest first, and directs every spare dollar at the most expensive debt while the rest receive only their minimums. Snowball orders the same balances by size, smallest first, and directs every spare dollar at the smallest balance regardless of interest rate. Both are defensible. They produce different outcomes, and the difference is rarely as large as either camp claims.

We get the question often enough that it is worth working out the answer once, slowly, on a single household. Below we use the canonical example that runs throughout this site — a credit card and a federal student loan, a fixed monthly allocation to debt, no surprises — and we publish the month-by-month verdict rather than the slogan.

What avalanche actually does

The avalanche method asks one question. Which dollar of your monthly debt payment buys you the most interest reduction? The answer, almost always, is the dollar that goes to the balance with the highest annual percentage rate. So you pay the minimums on every other balance to stay current, and you direct every additional dollar at the most expensive debt until it is gone. Then you redirect that whole stream at the next most expensive debt. The process repeats until there is nothing left to pay.

It is the strategy a treasurer would choose if asked to retire a portfolio of liabilities efficiently. It has no opinion about how it feels. It cares only about the interest the balance is generating, the dollars you can apply against it, and how long the arithmetic takes to bring the balance to zero.

What snowball actually does

The snowball method asks a different question. Which payoff event arrives soonest, regardless of cost? The smallest balance — whatever its rate — pays off first because you put your extra dollars there. When it closes, the minimum payment that was holding it open is freed and rolled into the next-smallest balance, which closes faster than it would have, and so on. The same total payment retires more accounts in the same time, even though it pays more interest along the way.

The argument for snowball is honest and empirical. People who pay down debt do not behave like balance sheets. They quit. They take on new debt. They lose the thread. A finished account, however small, is a piece of evidence that the strategy works, and that evidence sustains the next twelve months of discipline. If avalanche is the right answer for a calculator, snowball is sometimes the right answer for a person.

The math

The canonical household this site uses across every page has two debts. A credit card with a balance of $3,200 at an APR of 24.9% and an $80 minimum payment. A federal student loan with a balance of $5,380 at 6.1% and a $60 minimum. The household has decided it can put $410 a month toward debt — the $140 in combined minimums plus $270 in extra principal.

Under avalanche, the entire $270 in extra principal flows to the credit card first because 24.9% is more expensive money than 6.1%. The card closes earlier than it would under any other allocation; the moment it closes, the $350 that had been servicing it (the $80 minimum plus the $270 extra) folds into the student loan and finishes it off.

Under snowball, the same $270 flows to the credit card too — because it is also the smaller of the two balances. In this household the two methods agree on the first move, and almost agree on the second. That is the surprising part of working the example end to end. The bigger lesson is not the verdict; it is how close together the two answers sit when the smaller debt is also the more expensive one.

The published verdict that ships with the simulator on our debt-strategy page, with margins for rounding and the conventions the calculator uses, is this:

Verdict, this household

Avalanche pays in month 14, saves $487 in interest vs snowball.

Same monthly outlay either way. The cost difference is two semesters of a gym membership, paid to a credit card company.

If your two debts are larger, or further apart in rate, or further apart in size, the gap between the two methods opens. We have seen real households where avalanche saves the price of a used car against snowball, and we have seen households where the methods are within fifty dollars of each other. The only way to know is to run your own numbers.

When snowball wins anyway

The argument for paying the cheapest debt first is not financial. It is behavioural, and behavioural arguments are not a lesser kind of argument. They are the dominant variable for most households, because most households do not fail at debt payoff because the math is too hard. They fail because the timeline is too long and the early months feel like nothing is happening.

Snowball is the right method for you if any of these are true. You have stopped and restarted a debt-payoff plan more than once. You share the household budget with someone who reads bank statements rather than spreadsheets. You have small balances that are eating disproportionate mental energy. You can name, today, the date on which you stopped your last attempt, and the reason was discouragement rather than a shock to your income.

The $487 in this example is the price of the behavioural insurance, paid up front, in exchange for an earlier finished account. That is not a foolish trade. The household that finishes a payoff plan ten years from now using snowball is better off than the household that abandons an avalanche plan in month nine.

What we recommend

  • Run the numbers on both methods first. Use our debt-strategy tool, or any honest calculator. If the gap is under $500, the methods are interchangeable; pick the one you will actually finish.
  • If you are choosing between methods, choose the one your partner can read on a single page. A plan that fits on an index card and gets followed beats a plan that wins on a spreadsheet and gets quietly dropped.
  • Revisit the choice every six months, not every month. Switching strategies mid-payoff is the most expensive thing you can do, because it resets the psychological clock without changing the math.
