Why net worth is the only metric that matters

Income is a rate. Savings rate is blind to debt. One number nets all three — and it is the one most households never write down.

By the ProFinanceCast team 6 min read 27 April 2026

If we are allowed only one number to describe a household's financial situation, we ask for net worth. Not income, not savings rate, not credit score, not the balance of a particular account. Net worth, computed today, written down, and compared with the same number computed six months ago. Every other figure people commonly cite about themselves is a piece of a story; net worth is the story's ending so far.

This piece explains why. It also explains why we put a net-worth chart at the top of every authenticated screen in our forecasting tool rather than burying it three clicks deep, and what changes when you make this number the headline of your own financial life.

Why income is a rate, not a position

Income tells you how fast water is flowing into the bucket. It does not tell you how much water is in the bucket, and it does not tell you how big the hole in the bottom is. A household earning $200,000 a year and spending $210,000 a year is poorer at the end of the year than a household earning $60,000 and spending $55,000. Anyone who has worked in a wealth-management office has seen the high-income, low-position household; anyone who has read a few decades of household-finance research has seen the opposite case.

The conversational shorthand — "she earns six figures, so she must be fine" — treats income as if it were a stock when it is a flow. A flow can be turned off by a layoff, a health event, a divorce, or a market correction in your industry. The bucket, whatever is in it, is what you have on the day the flow stops. Income is a clue to net worth, not a substitute for it.

Why savings rate is blind to debt

Savings rate is a better metric than income because it is at least about a behavioural decision rather than a market wage. But it is blind to the other half of the balance sheet. A household putting 20% of a paycheque into a retirement account, while running a $15,000 credit-card balance at 24% APR, is not saving twenty percent of anything. The retirement account is growing at, charitably, the market rate. The card balance is growing at twenty-four percent. The household's net financial position is going backwards while the savings rate looks excellent on a dashboard.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the modal case for the audience this site was built for — mid-career professionals with retirement accounts of meaningful size and consumer debt that nobody talks about over dinner. The savings-rate metric flatters that household, and the flattery is the problem.

Why net worth nets it all

Net worth is the simplest definition in personal finance. Assets minus liabilities. Everything you own at honest market value, minus everything you owe. It cannot be flattered by a high salary, because the salary is not on the balance sheet until it has been earned, taxed, and either spent or saved. It cannot be flattered by a high savings rate, because the saving is not real until the debt against the savings has been paid off. It is the metric your future self would compute about your past self with no marketing department involved.

The canonical household this site uses across every page has a net worth of $87,420 today. Liquid and retirement assets total $96,000; total liabilities are $8,580. The number is the difference, and the difference is what compounds into the ten-year forecast we publish on the dashboard. A household with the same income, the same savings rate, and a different debt position would have a different net worth, a different forecast, and a different set of options.

Two objections we hear

The first objection is that net worth is volatile. Markets move; house prices move; the number printed on your balance sheet at the end of a quarter is not the number you will see at the end of the next quarter. This is true and also irrelevant. Net worth is a measurement, not a target. You write it down quarterly to learn the trend; you do not chase the number monthly. The smoothing handles itself if you stop staring at it.

The second objection is that net worth is reductive. A household with $87,420 in net worth could be a freelancer with no children, a couple with two kids and a mortgage, a graduate student with a trust, or a retiree drawing down savings. The number is the same; the lives are not. Also true, and again irrelevant for the purpose of the metric. Net worth is not a description of your life. It is a thermometer for one specific question — "is the line going up?" — and that question is worth answering regardless of the rest of the picture.

What to write down

Once a quarter, on a date you choose, write down four numbers on a single sheet of paper. The total value of everything you own at honest market value. The total of everything you owe. The difference, which is your net worth. The change since the last time you wrote it down. That is the entire methodology. It takes less than an hour the first time and less than fifteen minutes after that.

If the difference is positive and growing, your strategy is working and you should change nothing. If the difference is positive and flat, your strategy is treading water and the next quarter is a chance to investigate why. If the difference is negative or declining, you have your headline for the year and the rest of the questions order themselves. No metric on the dashboard answers this many questions with this little arithmetic. We recommend writing it down today.
