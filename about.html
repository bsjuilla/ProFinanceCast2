About

Why we built a forecasting house.

We don’t aggregate your bank accounts. We don’t sell ads. We don’t sell you. We project the next decade from four numbers.

Section 01

Our position.

ProFinanceCast is a boutique forecasting house, based in Europe, independent. We are not a bank, not a broker, not an aggregator, and not venture-backed. We have no investors to answer to, no integrations to defend, and no advertisers in the margins.

That posture is the product. Personal-finance software, as it stands, asks you to surrender your bank credentials to a third party so it can categorise transactions you already understand. We took a different route: type four numbers, and we project the next ten years from them. The math is published, the assumptions are dated, and the data stays in the browser you opened.

We write for an audience that prefers a quarterly letter to a notification. The register is plain, the figures are tabular, the palette is three. We’d rather show our work than win the argument.

Section 02

What we refuse to do.

The shape of a product is decided as much by what it omits as by what it ships. Four omissions, made deliberately, before the first line of code.

  • Bank login.

    We will not ask for credentials to a financial institution, ever. No Plaid, no Yodlee, no MX. The forecast takes four numbers and projects from there; the integration layer is the visitor’s memory.

  • Selling your data.

    Numbers entered into ProFinanceCast are computed in your browser. We do not warehouse, enrich, profile, or resell them. The business model is a subscription and a one-time licence — nothing else.

  • Manufactured urgency.

    No countdown timers, no exit-intent popups, no “three people are looking at this plan.” If the work is good, the pace can stay quiet.

  • Paid ads.

    We do not buy keywords, retarget visitors, or run growth-loop creative against you. The site is read in full or it is not read; either is acceptable.

Section 03

How we forecast.

The forecast is a deterministic projection, not a prediction. From four inputs — monthly income, monthly expenses, current debts, and current savings — we build a 120-month ledger that advances one month at a time. Income compounds at an assumed annual growth rate; expenses inflate at a separate rate; debts accrue interest at their stated APR and amortise against any allocated payment; savings compound at the long-run retirement rate.

Each month is computed from the previous month’s closing balance. Allocations follow a priority rule: minimum debt payments first, then the visitor’s chosen strategy (avalanche, snowball, or a custom mix), then retirement contributions, then liquid savings. Tax is applied to gross income via a country-specific table before any allocation runs. The output is a month-by-month series, which the charts draw and the tables summarise.

Nothing in the model is calibrated to a particular outcome. Change a single input and the entire 120-month ledger redraws. Where we make an assumption — growth rate, inflation, retirement compounding — the figure is published below and revised in the changelog when we revise it. We’d rather be wrong in public than right in private.
